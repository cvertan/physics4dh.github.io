Electromagnetic spectrum

The electromagnetic spectrum is complete range of all types of electromagnetic radiation from the shortest waves (gamma rays) to the longest (radio waves).

The electromagnetic radiation is classified by wavelength into types. Radio waves, microwaves, infrared rays, visible light, ultraviolet rays, X-rays, and gamma-rays are all types of electromagnetic radiation. All of them have different properties due to different values of wavelengths, frequencies and photon energies. The division into types is conditional, so that the spectral regions of different types of radiation overlap somewhat with each other.

The electromagnetic spectrum represents extremely large range of frequency and wavelengths values together with positions and names of various parts of electromagnetic spectrum. Going from low frequencies of radio waves (less than 108 Hz) to those of visible light (around 1015 Hz) represents increase in frequency by a factor of a billion. Similar is going from the frequency values of ultraviolet light (around 1016 Hz) to the very much higher ones of gamma rays (more than 1019 Hz).

In spectroscopy, a spectrum shows how much light at certain wavelength is emmitted, reflected or transmitted from the object. Correspondingly, there are emission, reflectance and transmission spectra of the object. Just like a fingerprint can be used to identify a person, the spectrum can be used to identify material.

To split the incoming light into a spectrum, a spectrometer is used.
