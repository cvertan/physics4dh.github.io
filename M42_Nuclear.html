Nuclear methods

Overview

Nuclear physics research methods play a significant role in the field of cultural heritage, offering valuable insights into the composition, origin, and preservation of various artifacts and archaeological materials.

The most used nuclear physics techniques are particle induced X-ray emission (PIXE), particle induced gamma emission (PIGE), X-rays fluorescence (XRF), Rutherford backscattering spectroscopy (RBS). These non-invasive techniques are widely used for characterizing inorganic materials.

Nuclear technology helps to collect information on age, composition and geographic origins of the artifacts. Source: www.foronuclear.org

Research tasks

The most common research tasks for applying nuclear physics methods are:

  • determining elemental compositions as major constituents or as trace elements;
  • creating elemental mapping for revealing hidden paintings;
  • set up for elemental mapping of large areas in a reasonable time for data taking;
  • near-surface investigations with micrometer resolution;
  • basic research on nanometer sized metallic inclusions in glasses;
  • high-quality imaging to ‘look behind’, ‘look inside’ or ‘look underneath the surface’.

By employing nuclear research methods in cultural heritage, scientists can gain insights into the materials, techniques, and historical context of artifacts and archaeological sites. These methods provide valuable scientific data for conservation, authentication, and historical interpretation, helping preserve and understand our cultural heritage.

Principle

The principle of nuclear physics research methods revolves around the interaction of radiation, such as X-rays, neutrons, protons, or gamma rays, with the atoms of a material, enabling the analysis of elemental composition, dating of materials, and the study of various physical properties of cultural artifacts, archaeological materials, and historical sites. Different types of radiation interact with matter in distinct ways, leading to characteristic responses that can be measured and analyzed.

In nuclear physics research methods, spectroscopy techniques and specialized detectors are used. Spectroscopy allows the measurement and analysis of the energy distribution of emitted radiation, providing insight into the composition and properties of the material. Detectors, such as X-ray detectors or gamma-ray spectrometers, capture and measure the emitted radiation, enabling quantitative analysis and data interpretation.
